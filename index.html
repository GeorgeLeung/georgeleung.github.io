About me


I am a Physics Ph.D. candidate at Emory University in Theoretical Biophysics. My research focuses on modelling of the behavior of animals in order to understand the sensorimotor system, more specifically, a roundworm called C. elegans. The goal of my research is to understand the individual and collective behavior of this worm. If we can do this, these models will greatly accelerate our knowledge of the functional structure of the sensorimotor neural system.
Contrary to the common picture of a theorist, my approach to the problem is very "dirty". Most of my work involve collecting massive datasets of worm behavior and building statistical models from them. That's why I am also interested in various machine learning and statistical modelling techniques.

Research Projects

Characterizing behavior of C. elegans

To understand the behavior of an animal, one must first find a way to describe it quantitatively. Previously, people use different ad-hoc measurements to analyze the behavior of the worm, which may miss out some important features. In this project, I build a computer vision system to track and analyze full-body behavior the worm. This system is able to differentiate subtle differences of different mutants.
Some of the results of this system are presented in this paper.

Inferring pain from C. elegans behavior

Studying pain on C. elegans is difficult since we are not able to tell how painful the worm is. In this project, I built the first ever model to infer the perceived pain level from the behavior of the worm. This model allows us to identify the effects caused by pain, and calculate the effective reduction of pain of the worm resulting from chemicals or genetic mutation.
This paper is submitted to PLoS Computational Biology and can be found on Arxiv. I also presented this project at APS march meeting 2016.
